Names Matter

Dec 14, 2017 4 min read

Now more than ever, I try to add an element of programming in most courses. Content can range from simple function calls in R to using dplyr as part of probability simulation. At some point in each of course, I find myself making two points about variable names.

Point 1: Names don’t matter.

It is important for students to know when they are required to use specific names (e.g. built-in functions or functions imported from a library) and when they have the power to pick their own names.

When programming in Python, I have developed a set of naming habits. For example, if I want to compute the monthly pay for a list of salaries, I would probably write the following.

monthly_pay  = [salary/12 for salary in salaries]

Over the years, I have found that some students are confused by my naming habits and will begin to think that they need to use the name row when working with a table, which is the thrust of the first point: The programmer holds the power to select names.

It is entirely possible to perform the above computation as follows,

unicorn  = [bob/12 for bob in snowflakes]

and a mathematician would probably be happy with

z = [x/12 for x in y]

but that brings us to the second point.

Point 2: Names totally matter.

While the unicorn, z and monthly_pay all represent the result of the same computation, stylistically the first is much better than the second and third. While it is important that a programming student appreciate the freedom of name selection, it is even more important that they learn to use quality names. In Literate Programming, Donald Knuth made the following important point about writing programs.

Let us change our traditional attitude to the construction of programs: Instead of imagining that our main task is to instruct a computer what to do, let us concentrate rather on explaining to human beings what we want a computer to do.

The first step in communicating our intent to other programmers is to select appropriate names. This is why I believe that the loop variable for table should be rows or that a text consist of words. In fact, when working with nested data structures, the names should be related to the levels of abstraction.

Levels of abstraction

A wikipedia defines a level of abstraction as follows.

Computer science commonly presents levels (or, less commonly, layers) of abstraction, wherein each level represents a different model of the same information and processes, but with varying amounts of detail. Each level uses a system of expression involving a unique set of objects and compositions that apply only to a particular domain.

For example, when processing an image in Python, we might represent pictures as an array of RGB tuples. In this case, the image can be thought of as having the following levels.

The outer-most level is a matrix that consists of rows, followed by rows that consist of RGB tuples and the RGB tuples consist of 8 bit integers.
Knuth states that

The psychological profiling [of a programmer] is mostly the ability to shift levels of abstraction, from low level to high level. To see something in the small and to see something in the large.

Notice that an important part of of describing the levels is picking a good name for each level. By encouraging our students to use names that describe each level of abstraction, we are helping them practice the most important skill in programming: abstraction.

It’s ALL in a name!

In his answer to this question, stackoverflow user miraculixx gives the following simple definition of abstraction:

Abstracting something means to give names to things, so that the name captures the core of what a function or a whole program does.

Even simple programs can become complex, and the tool that a programmer wields in the battle against complexity is abstraction. With proper abstractions, a complex computation can be broken into small and easy to understand components. These components are then composed into higher level abstractions, which are also short and easy to understand. This process is applied over and over until the program is complete. The form of the abstraction, whether it be functional or object- oriented, is not as important as the ability to package our computations in bite-sized chucks.

And since applying abstractions is giving names to computations, writing quality programs is quite literally all in the names.
